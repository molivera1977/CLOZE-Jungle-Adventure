🌴 Lost in the Amazon
A story about courage, curiosity, and the rainforest — by Mr. O's Class AI
📖 Read carefully — questions unlock after 3 minutes
3:00 remaining

Maya had always dreamed of exploring the Amazon rainforest. When her father, a scientist, invited her on his research trip to Brazil, she could hardly contain her excitement. She packed her notebook, her compass, and her favorite pair of binoculars. She had been preparing for a trip like this her entire life.

On their first morning in the jungle, Maya woke before sunrise. The air was thick and humid, heavy with the smell of wet leaves and earth. Strange bird calls echoed through the trees as thin rays of sunlight began to filter through the thick canopy above. She stood very still and just listened, amazed at how alive everything sounded.

As she followed a narrow trail, Maya noticed how diverse the rainforest was. In just a few steps, she counted three types of colorful frogs, two kinds of flowering vines, and a spider monkey watching her from high in the branches. She scribbled notes as fast as she could. Her father had taught her that careful observation was the most important skill a scientist could have.

The deeper they walked into the jungle, the more dense the vegetation became. Enormous leaves — some as big as a dining table — blocked much of the remaining light. Roots twisted up from the ground like giant fingers, making each step treacherous. Maya had to watch every footfall carefully to avoid tripping.

Then it happened. Maya turned around and realized she could no longer see the research camp. Her heart began to race, but she remembered what her father had taught her: stay calm, observe your surroundings, and trust your training. She took three slow, deep breaths. Panicking, she knew, would only make things worse.

Maya looked for the sun to find her direction and listened for the sound of the nearby river. Her compass told her she had been walking southeast, so the camp had to be northwest. She turned herself around and began walking slowly but deliberately, checking her compass every few minutes. Each step was calculated and careful.

Along the way, she noticed a peculiar orange mushroom growing at the base of a tree. She stopped to sketch it in her notebook, adding the exact location and time. Even lost, she was still doing science. Her father had always said that a true researcher never stops being curious, no matter the circumstances.

After a tense twenty minutes, Maya heard voices and splashing water. She pushed through a wall of broad leaves and stepped into a clearing right next to the camp. Her father ran toward her, relieved. Maya grinned and held up her notebook — she had filled six whole pages with observations.

That evening, sitting by the campfire, Maya's father reviewed her notes with wide eyes. Her sketches were detailed and accurate. Her descriptions were clear and organized. He told her that her mushroom sketch might actually be a species that had never been documented before. Maya felt a rush of pride unlike anything she had ever experienced.

"I wasn't lost," she told her father with a smile. "I was just doing extra research." Her father laughed and pulled her close. Maya looked out at the dark jungle, listening to the sounds of thousands of creatures going about their lives. She already knew she would be back — and next time, she would be even more prepared.
